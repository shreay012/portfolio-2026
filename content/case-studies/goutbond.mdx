## Background

Goutbond is a curated marketplace focused on artisanal and locally-made goods. They had a beautifully designed catalogue and a passionate founding team — but their retention numbers were alarming: fewer than 18% of first-time buyers returned within 30 days.

Their CEO described the core tension: *"We have amazing sellers. We have buyers who love the idea. But there's no stickiness. People buy once and disappear."*

The hypothesis: they had built a **store** when they needed to build a **community**.

---

## Research Phase

### Quantitative audit

I started with four weeks of analytics deep-dive alongside their growth team:

- 82% of all users visited only the homepage and one category page before dropping off
- Average session depth: 2.1 pages — significantly below the 5.5 pages needed for a conversion
- 94% of successful purchases came from users who had engaged with at least one piece of seller content (story, video, or community post)
- The top 12% of users (who returned 5+ times) almost all followed at least 3 sellers

**The signal was clear**: connection to sellers, not just products, drove retention.

### Qualitative interviews (14 users)

I spoke with 8 repeat customers and 6 one-time buyers to understand the difference.

Repeat buyers described emotional connections: they knew a seller's name, could recall their story, felt like they were supporting someone. One-time buyers described the same experience as "just Amazon but smaller."

> *"I come back because I want to know how Maria's ceramic line is doing. I feel like I know her."*
— Repeat buyer, 22 purchases in 6 months

> *"I bought a cutting board. It was nice. But there was nothing pulling me back."*
— One-time buyer

---

## Design Strategy

The core insight shifted my entire approach: this wasn't a homepage problem or a category navigation problem. **Goutbond needed to redesign itself as a social platform that happens to sell things — not a store that happens to have sellers.**

This led to three major design pillars:

1. **Seller-first navigation** — Surfaces sellers and their stories as prominently as products
2. **Follow & feed mechanics** — A social layer that gives users a reason to return even when they're not buying
3. **Community discovery engine** — AI-curated recommendations that get smarter with engagement signals, not just purchase history

---

## Key Design Work

### Redesigned Homepage: Feed, not Catalogue

The old homepage was a standard grid of products. I redesigned it as a personalized feed — mixing new arrivals from followed sellers, community activity (comments, saves), and editorially curated collections.

First-time users saw a category + value-alignment quiz (3 questions) to seed the recommendation engine before showing the feed.

### Seller Profile Pages

Seller pages were completely reimagined from product listings to **mini-magazines**. Each seller got:
- A hero story section (supported by rich text + photos)
- A "behind the scenes" content area
- Their full catalogue with storytelling context
- A follow button with follower count
- Community tab showing buyer reviews, photos, and discussion

### Design System

Goutbond had no design system — every page was built ad hoc. I built a full Figma component library with:
- 80+ components across 6 categories
- Semantic color tokens (brand, semantic, interactive, surface)
- Typography scale with semantic naming
- Spacing and layout tokens
- Interactive states for all components
- Dark and light mode support

This system reduced design-to-development time by ~35% and ensured visual consistency across all 12 screens built after handoff.

### Mobile-First Re-Architecture

Over 73% of Goutbond's traffic was mobile, but the site was clearly designed desktop-first. I rebuilt the mobile information architecture entirely — prioritizing the feed experience, bottom navigation for key actions, and swipe-friendly product discovery.

## What I Learned

The most valuable lesson from Goutbond: **retention design and community design are the same discipline**. When you give people a reason to come back that isn't just "here's more stuff to buy," you unlock an entirely different relationship with the product.

The design system work also reinforced how much time teams waste rebuilding patterns. Two weeks of systematic component work saved months of downstream design and dev effort — and made the product feel cohesive for the first time.
