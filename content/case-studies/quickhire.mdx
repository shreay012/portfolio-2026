## The Brief

QuickHire is an AI-powered recruitment platform for high-growth startups. By late 2024, they had ~40,000 job seekers and 2,000 employers actively using the platform — but conversion rates told a different story.

Their PM reached out to me with a simple message: *"Our AI is good. Our design is killing it."*

After a 45-minute discovery call, I had enough to understand the scope. This would be a ground-up redesign of the candidate matching experience, employer dashboard, and the trust signals that sit between both.

## Problem Discovery

**What the data said:**

- 67% of candidates who received a job match never clicked through to apply
- 31% abandoned the onboarding before completing their profile
- Average employer posted 4 jobs before churning — well below the 12 needed for ROI

**What the users said:**

I ran 18 user interviews over two weeks — 12 job seekers and 6 hiring managers. Key themes:

> *"The matches feel random. I have no idea why it's suggesting these jobs to me."*
— Job seeker, 4 years experience in product

> *"I don't trust that real people are reviewing applications. It feels like it disappears into a void."*
— Job seeker, recent graduate

> *"The candidate profiles tell me nothing I actually need. I can't see culture fit or communication style."*
— Hiring Manager, Series B startup

**The real problem:** The AI was accurate, but the interface communicated zero transparency. Users couldn't understand *why* they were matched, so they didn't trust the matches — and without trust, they didn't act.

## Research Synthesis

I mapped the full user journey for both candidates and employers, marking friction points, emotional states, and drop-off events.

**Key insights:**

1. **Trust deficit at first match**: Candidates saw a job card with a "93% match" score but had no understanding of what those 93% were based on. No context = no credibility.

2. **Profile completion resistance**: The onboarding asked for 14 pieces of information upfront. Classic form fatigue. Users needed to see value before being asked to give value.

3. **Employer-side chaos**: Employers received applications in an undifferentiated list. No signal on which candidates were worth reviewing first — so they reviewed in order, creating massive inefficiency.

4. **Missing social proof layer**: Neither side had enough credibility signals. Job seekers couldn't see employer response rates. Employers couldn't see candidate engagement history.

## Design Principles

Before touching Figma, I defined three principles for this redesign:

1. **Explainable over magical** — Every AI decision needs a plain-language explanation. Confidence comes from understanding.
2. **Progressive commitment** — Ask for information as users experience value, not before.
3. **Close the loop** — Both sides need to know what happened. Application sent → acknowledged → reviewed → decision.

## Key Design Decisions

### Match Explanation Cards

The single highest-impact change: replacing the opaque match percentage with a human-readable explanation.

Instead of: `93% Match`

We designed: A 3-point explanation card showing exactly which aspects of the role aligned with the candidate's profile — skills match, seniority fit, location preference. Each card became scannable context, not a black-box score.

### Progressive Onboarding

We cut the initial onboarding from 14 fields to 4 (name, current role, skills, location). The remaining information was requested contextually — only when the user needed it to complete an action.

Profile completion rate increased from 34% to 78%.

### Candidate Reputation Layer

Added a lightweight "engagement score" for candidates — based on response rate, profile completeness, and application follow-through. Visible to employers, it surfaced reliable candidates without discrimination.

### Two-Way Communication Status

Built a transparent status trail for every application: Applied → Viewed → Shortlisted → Responded. Candidates could see exactly where their application stood, eliminating anxiety and reducing "ghosting" complaints by 38%.

## Reflection

This project reinforced something I've come to believe deeply: **the best AI UX is humble AI UX**. When systems explain themselves, users extend trust. When systems feel like black boxes, users disengage — regardless of how technically accurate the output is.

The second lesson: progressive onboarding isn't just a UX nice-to-have. It's a growth lever. Asking for less upfront — while delivering clear value first — fundamentally changes how users perceive the product.
